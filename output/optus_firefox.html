    nbn® Home Internet FAQs

    What is the nbn®?

    nbn stands for national broadband network. It’s an internet network that is funded by the Australian Federal Government and built by NBN Co Ltd. Optus is one of Australia’s leading nbn service providers.

    What are the different nbn technology types?

    Fibre to the Premises (FTTP)

    FTTP is a term for the type of infrastructure used to connect your home or business to the internet. An nbn FTTP connection relies on a fibre optic line run from the nearest available node directly to your premises. Read nbn Fibre to the Premises (FTTP) Explained to learn more.

    nbn Hybrid Fibre Coaxial (HFC)

    nbn HFC, sometimes called nbn Cable, is a type of fixed line technology that can connect your premises to the nbn. A fibre cable is run to a nearby node, then existing cable TV wires complete the connection to your home. Read nbn Cable (HFC) Explained to learn more.

    Fibre to the Node (FTTN)

    Fibre to the Node (FTTN) enables data services and broadband connections to run via a central network box, which is referred to as a node. A fibre cable is run to a location on your street and then copper wiring connects you the rest of the way. Read nbn Fibre to the Node (FTTN) Explained to learn more.

    Fibre to the Building (FTTB)

    Fibre to the Basement (FTTB), sometimes called Fibre to the Building, is typically how larger buildings are connected on a fixed line to the nbn. A fibre cable connects to the building and then existing copper wiring is used the rest of the way. Read nbn Fibre to the Basement (FTTB) Explained to learn more.

    Fibre to the Curb (FTTC)

    A fibre cable is run close to your premises, usually the curb, and then copper wiring carries the connection the rest of the way. Read nbn Fibre to the Curb (FTTC) Explained to learn more.

     

    Do I need permission to install nbn® if I rent?

    Yes, you’ll need approval from your landlord or property manager because nbn installation may involve work on the property, such as drilling, cabling, or attaching equipment to building walls or fittings. 

    If you haven’t received the necessary approval, the installation will not proceed. 

    To make this easier, download this Renters Permission Template and send it to your landlord or property manager, so they can complete and return to you. 

    We recommend getting written approval before booking your appointment to ensure a smoother process.

    Can I upgrade my nbn?

    We have some great news. If you are an Optus customer with a Fibre to the Node (FTTN) or Fibre to the Curb (FTTC) nbn connection, and you live in an eligible area, your address may qualify for an nbn technology upgrade. To learn more, click here.

    What is the Optus Ultra WiFi Booster?

    The Optus Ultra WiFi Booster is a device that extends your existing WiFi coverage to help you get wall to wall, uninterrupted coverage within your home. It works by connecting with your compatible Optus modem to extend the WiFi coverage area of your home.

    What is the nbn® speed upgrade from September 2025?

    From September 2025, NBN Co will begin making some of their speed tiers even faster, supercharging how you connect, stream and work - and we will begin to automatically upgrade eligible Optus nbn plans on FTTP (Fibre to the Premises) and HFC (Hybrid Fibre Coaxial) to faster speeds – at no extra cost.

    That’s more speed for the same price. It’s internet that’s ready for smoother streaming, more reliable video calls, faster file uploads and gaming that keeps up with you.

    How does the nbn® speed upgrade affect Optus plans?
    • Optus Home Fast speeds will increase from 100/18Mbps to 500/43Mbps*.
    • Optus Home Superfast speeds will increase from 250/22Mbps to 680/43Mbps*.
    • Optus Home Ultrafast upload speed will increase from 800/40Mbps to 800/80Mbps*

    * Typical Busy Period Speeds (7-11pm). HFC and FTTP only.

    The Typical Busy Period Speeds advertised for the new nbn speed tiers are our best estimate, based on the available speed data. This is not based on the speeds observed for a representative sample (with the exception of the Ultrafast download speed which is not changing). Once more data is available, the Typical Busy Period Speeds for the new nbn speed tiers will be updated accordingly.

    What connection type is needed for the nbn® speed upgrade?

    The technology you're connected with matters when it comes to high speed internet. You'll need to either have a Fibre to the Premises (FTTP) or Hybrid Fibre Coaxial (HFC) connection to access faster speeds. 

    Other connection types, including Fibre to the Node (FTTN) and Fibre to the Curb (FTTC), are not eligible for the speed increases. However, as nbn are rolling out FTTP across Australia, you may be eligible for the nbn $0 Fibre Upgrade program (standard installation only). Use our eligibility checker and enter your address to find out. 

    Is your modem compatible for the nbn® speed upgrade?

    Your current modem or router, and how it is set up in your premises, could limit you from utilising the maximum speed of your internet plan.

    • For the new Fast speed tier, we recommend a modem that supports Wi-Fi 5 or higher
    • For the new Superfast and Ultrafast speed tiers, a Wi-Fi 6 capable modem is recommended

    Optus nbn plans include a powerful WiFi 6 modem, fully compatible so you can access the faster speeds, at no extra cost if you stay connected for 24 months.

    I set up the Optus Ultra WiFi Booster, now some of my devices can’t connect to the internet at all anymore, why?

    If you previously had devices connected to 5GHz band, you may need to reconnect them to your WiFi after your Booster has been connected. This is due to the enablement of Band Steering on your modem. Band Steering selects the best band based on the needs of each device. See ‘What is Band Steering?’ and ‘How does Band Steering work?’ below for more information.

    To reconnect your devices to your WiFi, use the WiFi login details printed on your modem label or Optus supplied fridge magnet. If you had previously customised your WiFi name or passphrase for the 2.4GHz band, this will still be your WiFi login, so do not use the old login that’s printed on the modem label or fridge magnet.

    What is Band Steering?

    Dual band modems like the Optus-supplied modems run two different frequency bands. The two bands have a signal of 2.4GHz or 5GHz. The difference between the two bands are: 

    -  2.4GHz has a longer range so it can cover more of an area. However it is slower and is more susceptible to interference from certain household appliances such as microwaves, cordless phones, Bluetooth devices or even your neighbours’ WiFi networks. 

    -  5GHz is faster than 2.4GHz, less susceptible to interference and is better for real-time apps and gaming. However, it has a shorter range than the 2.4GHz band. 

    When Band Steering is enabled, your modem will always try to connect you to the 5GHz band provided the signal strength is good and your device has 5GHz capability. To enable Band Steering you must have Single SSID enabled. See “What is a Single SSID?” below for more information.

    How does Band Steering work?

    Your WiFi enabled devices at home such as your laptop, smart phone, smart speakers all look for WiFi networks. Depending on the age or type of these devices, they may only support the 2.4GHz frequency. 

    Knowing what band is best for you to connect your device to can be difficult. This is where Band Steering from your Optus modem comes in. Band Steering will talk to your device to check the signal strength and if the device supports the 5GHz band. If signal strength is good and 5GHz is supported, it will connect to that frequency. If 5GHz is not supported or if the signal strength is poor, it will connect to 2.4GHz instead. 

    For example, if you have a new laptop and you connect it to the WiFi, Band Steering will automatically connect your laptop to the 5GHz frequency. Alternatively, if you have purchased some smart light bulbs for your home, these lights may only support 2.4GHz, so Band Steering will select this frequency, rather than trying to connect to 5GHz only for the connection fail.

    Why can I only see one WiFi band? I thought most modems have two.

    Optus has updated our WiFi modems to include Single SSID (see ‘What is SSID?’ below). This eliminates the need to have two WiFi bands. Smart Band Steering helps manage your devices by automatically selecting the WiFi band that works best for each device and will swap bands when it needs to.

    What is Single SSID?

    The Optus Ultra WiFi Booster uses Single SSID (SSID stands for Service Set Identifier). Commonly, SSID refers to the WiFi network you connect your devices to when connecting to the internet. 

    Most modems today are dual band modems meaning they have two frequencies, 2.4GHz and a 5Ghz. With most dual band modems, when you go to connect your devices you will see two SSIDs, for example MyOptusModem and MyOptusModem_5GHz. Single SSID combines both these WiFi names into one and allows for Band Steering to be enabled. 

    When you connect to the Single SSID, the modem or booster will automatically connect your device to the best frequency band, usually based on signal strength via the Band Steering function. See ‘What is Band Steering?’ and ‘How does Band Steering work?’ above for more information.

    CSG Waiver

    What is the Customer Service Guarantee (CSG) Standard?

    The Customer Service Guarantee Standard (CSG Standard) is an industry regulation that protects consumer customers from poor service. It includes a set of performance standards relating to connection, repair and appointment timeframes that Optus and other providers must meet when providing a standard telephone service to consumer customers with up to 5 fixed-line telephony services. 

    What is the CSG Waiver?

    The CSG Standard includes rules that a Carriage Service Provider must follow to allow for a customer to waive some or all of their protections under the Standard (CSG Waiver). From 12 September 2022, Optus has required customers to consent to a CSG Waiver prior to purchasing a fixed-line telephone service, in exchange for a better value service. The rights, protections and performance standards generally provided under the CSG that will be waived include: 

    • maximum timeframes for the connection of a standard telephone service and/or any enhanced call features;
    • maximum timeframes for the fixing of a fault with your standard telephone service and/or any enhanced call features;
    • timeframes for attending connection or fault fix appointments;
    • provision of information to you about these performance standards; and
    • prescribed compensation payable to customers if we are unable to meet these performance standards. 

    How will the CSG Waiver apply?

    Any customer who signs up or recontracts to an Optus standard telephone service will be required to consent to the CSG Waiver as a precondition to that purchase. 

    What does the CSG Waiver mean for how we support Optus customers?

    The CSG Waiver doesn't mean that we are not committed to providing you with the best customer service we can. As always, we'll endeavour to connect your service or fix any issues you have as fast as we can, with the best service possible. 

    What if I don't want to consent to the Customer Service Guarantee (CSG) Waiver?

    You don't have to agree to the CSG Waiver, but if you don't or if you withdraw consent within your cooling off period, then we'll decline to supply you with the Optus telephone service you ordered.

    You can still place an order for an internet service without telephony or an alternate product from us. You won't be able to place an order for Optus internet service with telephony without consenting to the CSG waiver.
